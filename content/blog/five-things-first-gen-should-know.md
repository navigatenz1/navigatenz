If you&apos;re the first in your family to head toward a New Zealand university, you&apos;re probably piecing the system together from scraps. Here are five things that consistently surprise first-gen families — and what to actually do about each one.

## 1. University Entrance is not the same as passing NCEA Level 3

This is the single biggest source of confusion we see. University Entrance (UE) is a separate threshold on top of NCEA Level 3.

To meet UE, you need:

- NCEA Level 3
- **Plus** 14 credits each in three UE-approved subjects at Level 3
- **Plus** 10 credits in numeracy at Level 1 or higher
- **Plus** 10 reading + writing credits at Level 2 or higher

Check our [UE guide](/guides/how-to-get-into-university) and use [the credit calculator](/tools/credit-calculator) to see exactly where you stand.

## 2. Universities use "rank scores" you&apos;ve probably never heard of

University of Auckland (and some programmes at other unis) uses a **rank score** — a weighted points system based on your Level 3 grades.

- Achieved = 2 points per credit
- Merit = 3 points per credit
- Excellence = 4 points per credit
- Calculated from your best 80 Level 3 credits → max rank of 320

Competitive programmes like Medicine and Law often need 250+. The [credit calculator](/tools/credit-calculator) works it out automatically.

## 3. StudyLink loans and allowances are two different things

Many families think "StudyLink" is one product. It&apos;s two:

- **Student Loan**: pays your tuition, up to a weekly living cost amount, plus $1,000 for course-related expenses. Interest-free while you&apos;re in NZ.
- **Student Allowance**: a weekly payment you don&apos;t pay back. Income-tested against your parents&apos; income if you&apos;re under 24.

If your combined parental income is under about $60k, you may get the full Student Allowance. Up to about $100–110k, a partial allowance. Above that, Student Loan only. Check the exact thresholds at [studylink.govt.nz](https://www.studylink.govt.nz).

## 4. Scholarships get left on the table every year

Many NZ scholarships go unfilled because nobody applied. Yours might be one. Use [our scholarship finder](/tools/scholarship-finder) to filter by year level, region, ethnic background, financial need, and field of study — you&apos;ll often find half a dozen you didn&apos;t know existed.

Tips from students who&apos;ve successfully applied:

- Apply early — some close in the last week of term 2.
- Ask your careers advisor and school dean for specific recommendations.
- Tell your story — community and iwi-based scholarships reward people who can articulate *why* university matters for them.

## 5. Moving out is probably cheaper than you think — and sometimes more expensive

Rules of thumb:

- **Living at home** is usually cheapest if your university is nearby — zero accommodation cost.
- **Halls** cost between $340 and $450 per week depending on the city, but include meals for first-year social life.
- **Flatting** is often cheaper weekly than halls but adds food + power + bond + finding a flat.

Dunedin is the cheapest student city in NZ. Auckland is the most expensive. Use [the hall vs flatting comparison](/tools/living-costs) for the specific city you&apos;re looking at.
